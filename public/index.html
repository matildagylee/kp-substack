What's actually in your perfume (and why no one talks about it)

I looked up the ingredients in my favourite fragrance. What I found was... not great.

Kristina P.

[ hero image: close-up of perfume bottles on a vanity, soft natural light ]

I have a confession.

I own a perfume I absolutely love. I've worn it for years. I get compliments on it constantly. It's the one product I never questioned.

And then one night I made the mistake of actually reading the ingredient list.

Or rather, trying to.

Because here's the thing about most perfumes: they don't really have an ingredient list. Not in any meaningful way. Where your moisturiser might list 20 or 30 identifiable compounds, your perfume probably just says "fragrance" or "parfum" and leaves it at that.

One word. Hiding potentially dozens of undisclosed chemicals.

And that one word is perfectly legal.

The "fragrance" loophole

In the US, the FDA doesn't require companies to disclose what's inside their fragrance formulations. The reasoning? Trade secret protection. A perfume house's scent formula is considered proprietary, like Coca-Cola's recipe. So instead of listing what's actually in the bottle, they're allowed to write "fragrance" and call it a day.

The EU is slightly better. They require disclosure of 26 known allergens if they're present above a certain concentration. But everything else? Still hidden behind that same word.

The average conventional perfume contains 20 to 40 synthetic chemicals that never appear on the label.

I'm not going to pretend I was calm about this. I went down a proper rabbit hole. And what I found made me rethink something I'd never given a second thought to.

What's hiding in there

When independent labs have tested popular fragrances, the same names keep showing up. Here's what you're likely spraying on your skin and breathing in every day:

Ingredient What it does Why it matters
Phthalates (especially DEP) Makes the scent last longer on skin Linked to hormone disruption, reproductive issues. Banned in EU cosmetics, still legal in US fragrances.
Synthetic musks (galaxolide, tonalide) Creates that "clean" base note Accumulates in body tissue and breast milk. Persistent environmental pollutant. Found in waterways globally.
Formaldehyde releasers (DMDM hydantoin, quaternium-15) Preserves the formula Slowly releases formaldehyde, a known carcinogen. Can trigger skin sensitisation and allergic reactions.
Styrene Synthetic base for certain scent profiles Classified as "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen" by the National Toxicology Program.
Benzyl benzoate Fixative and solvent Known skin sensitiser. Required to be disclosed in the EU above 0.001% in leave-on products, but often present without listing in the US.
Synthetic dyes Makes the perfume look pretty in the bottle Purely cosmetic. Some are derived from petroleum. Adds zero value to the scent itself.

Now. I want to be careful here. I'm not a toxicologist, and I'm not going to tell you your perfume is going to make you sick. The dose makes the poison, as they say.

But here's what bothers me: we don't get to decide for ourselves. We can't assess the dose because we don't even know what's in the bottle. You can't make an informed choice when the information is literally hidden behind a legal loophole.

We read the back of every food packet. We check our skincare ingredients. But perfume? Most of us have never even thought to look.

I certainly hadn't. And that's kind of the point.

What "clean fragrance" actually means (and what it doesn't)

Here's where it gets annoying. Because once you start looking for alternatives, you run into the word "clean" everywhere. And it doesn't mean anything.

There's no legal definition of "clean" in fragrance. No certification. No standard. A brand can slap "clean" on their marketing and still use synthetic musks, phthalates, and undisclosed fragrance compounds. Some do.

So what should you actually look for?

Full ingredient transparency. Every single component listed. Not "fragrance." Not "proprietary blend." The actual ingredients, by name.

ISO 9235 compliance. This is the international standard that defines what counts as a "natural aromatic raw material." If a brand references this, they're working with botanicals and plant-derived compounds, not synthetics labelled as natural.

No phthalates, no synthetic musks, no formaldehyde. These should be explicitly called out as absent, not just implied by the word "clean."

The honest trade-offs

I'm going to be real because that's the whole point of this newsletter:

Natural fragrances don't last as long. Where a synthetic perfume might carry you from 8am to midnight, a natural one fades within a few hours. The molecules are lighter. That's just chemistry. You'll need to carry it with you and reapply.

They're more expensive per ml. Plant extracts and essential oils cost more than lab-made synthetics. That's a fact. But you're also not spraying hormone disruptors on your wrists every morning, so there's a calculation to make.

The scent profile is different. If you love the specific sharp, bright, linear scent that synthetic fragrances deliver, natural alternatives might feel muted or "rounder" to you. They evolve differently on skin. I personally prefer that now. But it took some adjusting.

My take: Natural perfume costs more and lasts less. That's the honest truth. But for something I spray directly onto my skin and inhale all day, I've decided I want to know what's in it. Your calculation might be different. That's fine.

What I'd tell you if we were having coffee

You don't need to throw out your perfume collection tomorrow. I haven't. I still have a couple of conventional fragrances I wear occasionally.

But I'd tell you to do one thing: go look up the ingredients in your daily perfume. Not on the bottle - there won't be much there. Search for the brand's full ingredient list on their website or on a database like EWG's Skin Deep.

If you can find a full list? Great. Make your own call with open eyes.

If the brand won't tell you what's in the bottle? That tells you something too.
